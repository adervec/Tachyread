01

The foundation: touch typing

Fast typing rests on one principle: your fingers know where the keys are, so your eyes never leave the screen. This is touch typing. Hunt-and-peck typists plateau around 30–40 WPM because every glance down breaks rhythm and concentration. Touch typists routinely double that.

The home row is your anchor

Rest your left fingers on A S D F and your right fingers on J K L ;. The small bumps on F and J let you re-find this position without looking. Every other key is reached relative to home row — and your fingers always return home after each stroke.

02

Posture & ergonomics

You can't sustain speed from a cramped position, and poor form invites repetitive strain injury. Set your body up before you set your fingers loose.

Body

From the chair up

  • Feet flat on the floor, thighs roughly parallel to the ground.
  • Elbows at ~90°, close to your sides — not splayed out.
  • Wrists straight and floating, not bent up or resting hard on the desk while striking keys.
  • Shoulders relaxed; you are not bracing for impact.
Setup

The gear around you

  • Monitor at eye level, about an arm's length away, so you're not craning down.
  • Keyboard flat or slightly negative-tilted. Those little flip-out feet that raise the back? Most ergonomists say leave them down.
  • Light touch. Strike keys, don't pound them. Bottoming-out hard is slower and harsher on joints.
  • Micro-breaks: look away and stretch your hands every 20–30 minutes.
Rule of thumb

If your hands hurt, stop. Fatigue and pain destroy form long before they destroy speed. Comfortable beats fast every single time.

03

The method: accuracy first

This is the part most people get wrong. They try to type fast, make errors, backspace constantly, and never actually get faster. Backspacing is the real speed killer — every correction costs you the time to notice, the time to delete, and the time to retype.

The core training loop

  • Slow down until you're accurate. Find the speed where you make almost no mistakes, then live there.
  • Hold ~97% accuracy. Only when you can sustain that do you let speed creep up. Speed follows accuracy automatically; it does not work the other way around.
  • Type in rhythm, not bursts. A steady, even cadence beats frantic sprints followed by error-recovery. Aim for smooth, metronomic flow.
  • Never look down. If you don't know a key, glance, then force yourself to find it by feel next time. Cover your hands if you must.
  • Use all ten fingers, always. Even if a "two-finger shortcut" feels faster today, it caps your ceiling. Commit to correct fingering even while it's slower.
The mantra

Slow is smooth, and smooth is fast. Build the correct movement first; the speed is the reward for getting the movement right.

04

Targeted drills

General typing helps, but deliberate practice on your weak spots helps far more. Drill the specific things your fingers fumble.

Drill 01

Home-row locking

Type the home keys until they're automatic, returning fingers home each time:

asdf jkl; asdf jkl; fjdk sla; jfkd ls;a

Drill 02

Reach & return

Practice the up/down reaches without drifting off home:

ftf gtg jyj huh ded kik frf juj cdc m,m

Drill 03

Common bigrams

The letter-pairs English uses most. Make these one fluid motion:

th he in er an re on at en nd ti es or te

Drill 04

Weak-finger focus

Pinkies and ring fingers lag for almost everyone. Hammer them:

qaz wsx pl; oki que was zap pop lax saw

Drill 05

Numbers & symbols

The top row and shifted symbols are most people's blind spot:

1q2w 9o0p $5 &7 (8) #3 user@mail.com 50%

Drill 06

Capitalization & flow

Use the opposite-hand shift, then keep moving:

The Quick Fox. Anna And Ben. New York City.

How to drill

Pick one drill per session. Run it slowly and perfectly for 3–5 minutes before doing anything else. Identify the keys you keep missing, and make those tomorrow's drill.

06

A four-week practice plan

15–20 focused minutes a day beats two unfocused hours on the weekend. Consistency rewires muscle memory; cramming doesn't. Here's a progression you can repeat:

WeekFocusDaily session (~15–20 min)Goal
1Placement5 min home-row drills · 10 min slow accurate sentences, eyes upStop looking down. Lock home row.
2Coverage5 min reach drills (top/bottom rows) · 10 min full-paragraph practiceHit every letter by feel at ~97% accuracy.
3Fluency5 min bigram/weak-finger drills · 10 min real text (articles, emails)Smooth rhythm; numbers & punctuation included.
4Speed3 min warm-up · timed tests, nudging pace up while accuracy holdsLet speed rise without errors climbing.
Then loop

Re-run the cycle, each time starting from a higher baseline. Most people see a noticeable jump within 2–4 weeks of daily practice. Plateaus are normal — push through with targeted drills on your weakest keys.

07

Measuring progress

Formula

How WPM works

A "word" is standardized as 5 characters (including spaces), so "keyboard " counts the same regardless of the actual word.

Gross WPM = (characters ÷ 5) ÷ minutes

Net WPM subtracts your uncorrected errors — it's the number that actually matters, because it rewards accuracy.

Target

Accuracy is king

A blazing 90 WPM at 88% accuracy is often slower in practice than 70 WPM at 99%, once you account for fixing mistakes.

  • Aim for 97%+ accuracy as your floor.
  • Track net WPM, not gross, over time.
  • Test on real text, not just easy drills.

08

Common mistakes & fixes

✗ Looking at the keyboard

Fix: Cover your hands with a cloth, or use a keyboard with blank keycaps for practice. Force the recall.

✗ Chasing speed too early

Fix: Drop your pace until errors nearly vanish. Speed is a result, not an input.

✗ Wrong fingers (cheating)

Fix: Commit to the standard finger map even when it's slower. Bad habits cap your ceiling permanently.

✗ Resting wrists while striking

Fix: Float your hands. Wrist rests are for pauses, not for typing.

✗ Tension & pounding keys

Fix: Relax shoulders and hands; use a light, quick touch. Tension is slow and tiring.

✗ Practicing inconsistently

Fix: Short daily reps. 15 minutes a day beats sporadic marathons every time.

10

Tools to practice with

Pair this guide with a dedicated trainer that gives instant feedback. Look for ones that:

  • Report accuracy and net WPM, highlight your error-prone keys, and adapt drills to your weaknesses.
  • Offer lessons for beginners (structured home-row → full-keyboard progressions) if you're starting fresh.
  • Use real words and sentences, not just random letters, so your practice transfers to real writing.
  • Make it fun — typing games and short timed tests keep daily practice from feeling like a chore.

Popular free options worth a search include browser-based typing tutors and typing games; many also have an "analysis" mode that shows which keys slow you down most. The trainer matters less than showing up daily — pick one and stick with it.
